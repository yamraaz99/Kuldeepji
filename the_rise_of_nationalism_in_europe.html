The Rise of Nationalism in Europe: An Interactive Journey

1. Sorrieu's Vision & The Nation-State

The chapter begins with Frédéric Sorrieu's 1848 prints envisioning a world of ‘democratic and social Republics’. His first print shows the peoples of Europe and America marching past the Statue of Liberty, symbolising the fraternity among nations.

Absolutist

Rule with no restraints on power, often monarchical, centralised, militarised and repressive.

Utopian

A vision of society so ideal it's unlikely to exist in reality.

Nation-State

A state where the majority of citizens (not just rulers) share a common identity, history, or descent, often within a clearly defined territory under a central power.

Plebiscite

A direct vote by all people of a region to accept or reject a proposal.

Key Idea: Modern nationalism emerged in the 19th century, leading to the rise of nation-states replacing multi-national empires. This common identity was forged through struggles.

Ernst Renan's 'What is a Nation?' (1882)

Renan argued a nation is not just common language, race, religion, or territory. It's a "large-scale solidarity" based on a shared past of sacrifice, a common will in the present, and shared future goals. Its existence is a "daily plebiscite". Nations guarantee liberty.

2. The French Revolution and the Idea of the Nation

The 1789 French Revolution was the first clear expression of nationalism. It transferred sovereignty from the monarch to French citizens.

Creating Collective Identity:

  • Ideas of la patrie (fatherland) & le citoyen (citizen) emphasized a united community with equal rights.
  • New French tricolour flag replaced the royal standard.
  • Estates General renamed National Assembly (elected by active citizens).
  • New hymns, oaths, martyrs commemorated in the nation's name.
  • Centralised administration with uniform laws.
  • Internal customs duties abolished; uniform weights & measures adopted.
  • Regional dialects discouraged; Parisian French became the common language.
  • Declared mission: Liberate other European peoples from despotism (help them become nations).

Napoleon and Reforms:

Though Napoleon destroyed democracy, his administrative reforms spread revolutionary principles:

Napoleonic Code (Civil Code of 1804)

Abolished birth privileges, established equality before law, secured property rights. Exported to regions under French control (Dutch Republic, Switzerland, Italy, Germany). Abolished feudal system, freed peasants from serfdom, removed guild restrictions.

Initial welcome in places like Holland, Switzerland, Brussels, Milan turned to hostility due to increased taxation, censorship, forced conscription.

3. The Making of Nationalism in Europe

Europe Before Nation-States (Mid-18th C):

No nation-states as we know them. Germany, Italy, Switzerland were divided into kingdoms, duchies, cantons. Eastern/Central Europe had autocratic monarchies with diverse peoples lacking collective identity.

The Habsburg Empire (Example)

A patchwork ruling Austria-Hungary: Included Alpine regions (Tyrol, Austria, Sudetenland), Bohemia (German-speaking aristocracy), Lombardy & Venetia (Italian-speaking). Hungary had Magyar speakers and others. Galicia had Polish aristocracy. Also included Bohemians, Slovaks, Slovenes, Croats, Roumans. Only common tie was allegiance to the emperor.

Social Structures:

  • Aristocracy: Landed, dominant class. Spoke French in high society, connected by marriage. Numerically small.
  • Peasantry: Majority population. West: tenants/small owners. East/Central: serfs on vast estates.
  • New Middle Class: Emerged with industrialisation (West/Central Europe). Included industrialists, businessmen, professionals. Educated, liberal middle class drove ideas of national unity after abolishing aristocratic privileges.

Liberal Nationalism:

What it Stood For:

  • Individual freedom, equality before law.
  • Government by consent (representative parliament).
  • End of autocracy & clerical privileges, constitution.
  • Inviolability of private property.
  • Economic Sphere: Freedom of markets, abolition of state restrictions on movement of goods/capital.

Limitations:

  • Suffrage (right to vote) often limited to property-owning men initially. Women and non-propertied men excluded.

Zollverein (Customs Union) - 1834

Initiated by Prussia, joined by most German states. Abolished tariff barriers, reduced currencies (from over 30 to 2). Aimed to bind Germans economically, stimulate mobility (railways), and foster national sentiment.

A New Conservatism after 1815:

Congress of Vienna (1815)

Hosted by Austrian Chancellor Metternich. Aimed to restore monarchies overthrown by Napoleon (e.g., Bourbons in France), create a new conservative order, prevent French expansion. Resulted in territorial changes (Netherlands, Prussia, Austria, Russia gain territory). German confederation left untouched.

Conservative Regimes:

Autocratic, intolerant of criticism/dissent, imposed censorship to control ideas of liberty/freedom associated with French Revolution.

The Revolutionaries:

Fear of repression drove liberal-nationalists underground. Secret societies formed (e.g., Carbonari in Italy). Aimed to oppose monarchy, fight for liberty/freedom, and create nation-states.

Key Figure: Giuseppe Mazzini (Italian). Founded Young Italy & Young Europe. Believed in unified republics. Seen as a threat by conservatives like Metternich.

4. The Age of Revolutions: 1830-1848

Liberalism and nationalism became associated with revolution against conservative regimes.

Uprisings and Independence:

  • July 1830 (France): Bourbon kings overthrown, constitutional monarchy under Louis Philippe installed. Sparked uprising in Brussels -> Belgian independence from Netherlands.
  • Greek War of Independence (1821-1832): Against Ottoman Empire. Supported by West Europeans (inspired by ancient Greek culture). Poet Lord Byron fought. Greece recognized as independent (Treaty of Constantinople, 1832).

Romantic Imagination and National Feeling:

Culture (art, poetry, music, stories) played a key role in shaping nationalism.

Romanticism:

  • Cultural movement emphasizing emotion, intuition, mystical feelings (over reason/science).
  • Focused on shared collective heritage, common cultural past (basis of nation).
  • Johann Gottfried Herder (German): True culture found among common people (das volk). Promoted folk songs, poetry, dances (volksgeist - true spirit of nation).
  • Grimm Brothers (German): Collected folktales seen as pure German spirit, opposed French domination.
  • Poland: National feelings kept alive through music (Karol Kurpinski - polonaise, mazurka) and language (used as resistance against Russian occupation).

Hunger, Hardship and Popular Revolt:

1830s: Great economic hardship. Population increase > jobs. Rural migration to slums. Competition from cheap English machine goods (esp. textiles). Peasants struggled with feudal dues.

1848: Food shortages, unemployment led to Paris uprising; Louis Philippe fled; Republic proclaimed; suffrage for adult males; right to work guaranteed.

1845: Silesian weavers revolted against contractors cutting payments (described by Wilhelm Wolff).

1848: The Revolution of the Liberals:

Educated middle classes led parallel revolutions demanding constitutionalism with national unification (Germany, Italy, Poland, Austro-Hungarian Empire).

Frankfurt Parliament (Germany, May 1848)

831 elected representatives drafted constitution for German nation headed by monarchy subject to parliament. Offered crown to Friedrich Wilhelm IV (King of Prussia), who rejected it. Parliament lost support (opposed worker demands) and was forcibly disbanded.

The Feminist Issue:

Women participated actively (associations, newspapers, demonstrations) but were denied suffrage. Admitted only as observers in Frankfurt Parliament. Debates arose (e.g., Carl Welcker vs. Louise Otto-Peters) on women's political rights.

Outcome: Liberal movements suppressed, but monarchs realised concessions needed. Serfdom/bonded labour abolished in Habsburg dominions & Russia. Hungarians granted more autonomy (1867).

5. The Making of Germany and Italy & Britain

After 1848, nationalism often mobilised by conservatives for state power.

Germany Unified (1866-1871)

Liberal 1848 attempt failed. Prussia under Chief Minister Otto von Bismarck took leadership. Used Prussian army/bureaucracy ('iron and blood'). Three wars (vs Denmark, Austria, France) led to victory. Prussian King William I proclaimed German Emperor at Versailles (Jan 1871). Showed dominance of Prussian state power.

Italy Unified (1859-1870)

Long history of fragmentation. Only Sardinia-Piedmont ruled by Italian house. North under Austria, Centre under Pope, South under Spain. Mazzini's earlier republican efforts failed. Sardinia-Piedmont under King Victor Emmanuel II led unification via war. Chief Minister Cavour (pragmatic diplomat) allied with France to defeat Austria (1859). Giuseppe Garibaldi led volunteer army ('Red Shirts') conquering South (1860). Victor Emmanuel II proclaimed King of United Italy (1861). Rome added 1870. Many peasants unaware ('La Talia' = Victor Emmanuel's wife?).

The Strange Case of Britain

Nation-state not result of sudden revolution, but long process. No 'British nation' before 18th C. Primary identities ethnic (English, Welsh, Scot, Irish). English nation grew in wealth/power, extended influence. English Parliament (seized power 1688) forged nation-state. Act of Union (1707) with Scotland created 'United Kingdom of Great Britain'; English dominance, Scottish culture suppressed. Ireland (Catholic majority vs Protestants helped by English) forcibly incorporated in 1801 after failed revolt (Wolfe Tone). New 'British nation' forged via dominant English culture (flag, anthem, language).

6. Visualising the Nation: Allegories

How to give a nation a face? Artists in 18th/19th C personified nations as female figures (allegories) - not real women, but giving abstract idea concrete form.

Marianne (France)

Popular Christian name symbolising people's nation. Used symbols of Liberty (red cap, broken chain) & Republic (tricolour, cockade). Statues erected, images on coins/stamps.

Germania (German Nation)

Wears crown of oak leaves (German oak = heroism). Other symbols:

9. Nationalism and Imperialism

By the last quarter of the 19th century, nationalism often became a narrow creed with limited ends, intolerant and ready for war. Major European powers manipulated nationalist aspirations in regions like the Balkans to further their own imperialist aims.

The Balkans: Powder Keg of Europe

Region of geographic/ethnic variation (Romania, Bulgaria, Albania, Greece, Macedonia, Croatia, Bosnia-Herzegovina, Slovenia, Serbia, Montenegro - inhabitants broadly Slavs). Large part under Ottoman Empire control. Spread of romantic nationalism + Ottoman disintegration = explosive region. Balkan peoples claimed independence based on past history, struggled to define identity. Fiercely jealous of each other. Became scene of big power rivalry (Russia, Germany, England, Austro-Hungary) competing for control. Led to series of wars and finally WWI (1914).

Key Idea: Nationalism aligned with imperialism led Europe to disaster. However, anti-imperial movements in colonised countries also developed, inspired by nationalism and the desire for independent nation-states.

Napoleon Bonaparte

French general and emperor (ruled early 19th C).

Destroyed democracy in France but spread revolutionary administrative ideas (Napoleonic Code, 1804) across Europe, promoting efficiency, equality before law, property rights, and abolishing feudalism.

Duke Metternich

Austrian Chancellor (early-mid 19th C).

Leading conservative figure. Hosted Congress of Vienna (1815) to restore monarchy after Napoleon. Feared revolution and nationalism. Described Mazzini as 'most dangerous enemy'.

Giuseppe Mazzini

Italian revolutionary (1807-1872).

Founded 'Young Italy' & 'Young Europe'. Believed nations were natural units. Advocated for a unified Italian Republic. Opposed monarchy, inspired revolutionaries.

Otto von Bismarck

Chief Minister of Prussia (mid-late 19th C).

Architect of German unification via 'Realpolitik' and 'iron and blood' (military force), not liberalism. Used conservative nationalism for Prussian dominance.

Count Camillo de Cavour

Chief Minister of Sardinia-Piedmont (mid-19th C).

Pragmatic statesman key to Italian unification. Used diplomacy (alliance with France) and war to unify North/Central Italy under Victor Emmanuel II.

Giuseppe Garibaldi

Italian nationalist & military leader (1807-1882).

Led 'Red Shirts' in 1860, conquering Southern Italy. Embodied popular, romantic nationalism.

Louise Otto-Peters
