Nationalism in India: An Interactive Journey

Introduction: Nationalism and Anti-Colonialism

In India, as in many colonies, the growth of modern nationalism is closely linked to the anti-colonial movement. The shared experience of oppression under colonialism forged bonds of unity among different groups. However, experiences varied, and notions of freedom differed. The Congress under Mahatma Gandhi sought to unite these groups, though not without conflict.

This chapter covers the story from the 1920s, focusing on the Non-Cooperation and Civil Disobedience Movements, participation of various social groups, and how nationalism captured people's imagination.

1. The First World War, Khilafat and Non-Cooperation

Impact of World War I (Post-1919):

  • New economic/political situation: Huge increase in defence spending (financed by war loans, taxes).
  • Price rises (doubled 1913-18) caused extreme hardship.
  • Villages supplied soldiers; forced recruitment caused anger.
  • Crop failures (1918-19, 1920-21) led to food shortages.
  • Influenza epidemic killed millions (12-13 million perished from famines/epidemic per 1921 census).
  • Hardships continued after the war.

The Idea of Satyagraha:

Mahatma Gandhi returned from South Africa (Jan 1915) with the novel method of mass agitation: Satyagraha.

Core Principles:

  • Emphasised power of truth, need to search for truth.
  • If cause is true (against injustice), physical force is unnecessary.
  • Win through non-violence by appealing to oppressor's conscience.
  • Persuade, don't force acceptance of truth.
  • No ill-will; pure soul-force.

Early Satyagraha Movements:

  • 1917: Champaran (Bihar) - Against oppressive plantation system.
  • 1917: Kheda (Gujarat) - Supported peasants affected by crop failure/plague, demanding revenue relaxation.
  • 1918: Ahmedabad (Gujarat) - Organised movement amongst cotton mill workers.

The Rowlatt Act (1919):

Act hurriedly passed despite Indian opposition. Gave govt enormous power to repress political activities, allowed detention without trial for 2 years.

Gandhiji called for non-violent civil disobedience starting with a hartal (strike) on April 6.

Rallies, strikes, shop closures occurred. British clamped down; local leaders arrested; Gandhi barred from Delhi.

April 10: Police fired on peaceful procession in Amritsar.

April 13: Jallianwala Bagh Massacre. Crowd gathered (protest/Baisakhi fair). General Dyer blocked exit, ordered firing, killing hundreds. Aim: 'produce a moral effect' (terror).

Aftermath: Strikes, clashes, attacks on govt buildings. Govt responded with brutal repression (forced crawling, floggings, bombing villages). Gandhi called off the movement due to spreading violence.

Khilafat and Non-Cooperation:

Gandhi sought broader movement, needing Hindu-Muslim unity. Took up Khilafat issue: Defending temporal powers of Ottoman emperor (Khalifa) after WWI defeat.

Khilafat Committee formed (Bombay, March 1919). Leaders like Muhammad Ali and Shaukat Ali discussed united action with Gandhi.

Calcutta Congress (Sep 1920): Gandhi convinced leaders to start Non-Cooperation Movement for Khilafat and Swaraj (self-rule).

Why Non-Cooperation? (Gandhi's Hind Swaraj, 1909):

British rule established/survived due to Indian cooperation. Refusal to cooperate -> British rule would collapse.

Proposed Stages:

  • Surrender govt titles.
  • Boycott civil services, army, police, courts, councils, schools, foreign goods.
  • If repressed, launch full civil disobedience.

Nagpur Congress (Dec 1920): Non-Cooperation programme adopted after internal debate.

2. Differing Strands within the Movement (Jan 1921 onwards)

Various social groups participated, responding to call of Swaraj but interpreting it differently.

Movement in Towns:

  • Middle-class participation: Students left govt schools/colleges, teachers/headmasters resigned, lawyers gave up practice.
  • Council elections boycotted (except Madras - Justice Party).
  • Economic effects: Foreign goods boycotted, liquor shops picketed, foreign cloth burnt. Imports halved (1921-22). Indian textile production increased.
  • Slowdown reasons: Khadi expensive, lack of alternative Indian institutions (schools, courts). People trickled back.

Rebellion in the Countryside:

Awadh Peasants:

Led by Baba Ramchandra (ex-indentured labourer). Against talukdars/landlords demanding high rents, cesses, begar (forced labour without pay).

Demands: Reduce revenue, abolish begar, social boycott landlords. Nai-dhobi bandhs organised.

Jawaharlal Nehru involved; Oudh Kisan Sabha set up (Oct 1920).

Movement turned violent (1921): Houses attacked, bazaars looted. Local leaders invoked Gandhi's name for actions Congress disapproved of.

Gudem Hills Tribals (Andhra Pradesh):

Militant guerrilla movement (early 1920s). Against forest laws preventing entry for grazing/collecting fuel/fruits, and forced begar for road building.

Led by Alluri Sitaram Raju (claimed special powers, seen as incarnation of God). Inspired by NCM, persuaded wearing Khadi/giving up drinking BUT asserted force needed for liberation.

Rebels attacked police stations, attempted killing officials. Raju captured/executed (1924), became folk hero.

Swaraj in the Plantations (Assam):

Workers interpreted Swaraj as right to move freely (restricted by Inland Emigration Act 1859) and retain link with village.

Hearing of NCM, thousands defied authorities, left plantations heading home (believing Gandhi Raj coming, land in villages).

Caught due to strikes, brutally beaten.

Vision of movements often differed from Congress programme. People interpreted Swaraj in own ways, but related emotionally to all-India agitation by invoking Gandhi's name.

3. Towards Civil Disobedience

Feb 1922: Gandhi withdrew NCM after Chauri Chaura incident (violent clash).

Internal Debates & Factors Shaping Politics (late 1920s):

  • Some leaders (e.g., C. R. Das, Motilal Nehru) tired of mass struggle, formed Swaraj Party to enter councils, oppose British policies from within.
  • Younger leaders (Nehru, Subhas Chandra Bose) pressed for radical mass agitation, full independence.
  • Worldwide economic depression effect: Agricultural prices fell (from 1926, collapsed post-1930). Peasants struggled to sell harvests/pay revenue. Countryside in turmoil.
  • Simon Commission (under Sir John Simon): Constituted by Tory govt (Britain) to look into constitutional system. No Indian members -> widespread protests ('Go back Simon') when arrived (1928).

Towards Purna Swaraj:

  • Lord Irwin's vague offer (Oct 1929) of 'dominion status' and Round Table Conference didn't satisfy Congress.
  • Lahore Congress (Dec 1929): Under Nehru's presidency, formalised demand for 'Purna Swaraj' (full independence). Declared Jan 26, 1930 as Independence Day (pledge taken).

The Salt March and Civil Disobedience Movement (CDM):

Gandhi found powerful symbol in salt tax (affected rich/poor). Sent letter to Irwin (Jan 31, 1930) with 11 demands (general/specific, including abolishing salt tax). Ultimatum: launch CDM if demands not met by March 11.

Irwin unwilling to negotiate.

Salt March (Dandi March): Started March 12, 1930. Gandhi + 78 volunteers walked 240 miles (Sabarmati to Dandi). Reached Dandi April 6, violated salt law by making salt.

Marked beginning of Civil Disobedience Movement: People asked to break colonial laws (unlike NCM's refusal to cooperate). Salt law broken nationwide, foreign cloth boycotted, liquor shops picketed, peasants refused revenue/chaukidari taxes, village officials resigned, forest laws violated.

Govt arrested leaders (Abdul Ghaffar Khan, Gandhi). Led to violent clashes (Peshawar, Sholapur). Brutal repression: satyagrahis attacked, women/children beaten, ~100,000 arrested.

Gandhi called off movement, entered Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5, 1931): Agreed to participate in Second Round Table Conference (London), govt agreed release political prisoners.

Gandhi went to London (Dec 1931), negotiations broke down. Returned to find new repression cycle (Ghaffar Khan, Nehru jailed, Congress illegal).

Relaunched CDM. Continued over a year, lost momentum by 1934.

4. How Participants Saw the Movement & Limits of CDM

Varied Participation & Ideals:

Rich Peasants (e.g., Patidars of Gujarat, Jats of UP):
Active supporters. Hard hit by depression/falling prices. Swaraj = struggle against high revenues. Disappointed when CDM called off (1931) without revenue revision; reluctant to rejoin (1932).
Poor Peasants:
Wanted lower revenue + unpaid rent remitted. Joined radical movements (Socialist/Communist led). Congress unwilling to support 'no rent' campaigns (feared alienating rich peasants/landlords). Relationship uncertain.
Business Classes (e.g., Purshottamdas Thakurdas, G. D. Birla):
Reacted against colonial policies restricting business. Wanted protection against imports, favourable rupee-sterling ratio. Formed FICCI (1927). Supported CDM initially (financial aid, refused import trade). Swaraj = end of colonial restrictions. Enthusiasm waned after RTC failure (feared militant activities, socialism).
Industrial Working Classes:
Did not participate in large numbers (except Nagpur). Stayed aloof as industrialists came closer to Congress. Some participated selectively (boycott, strikes against low wages/poor conditions). Congress reluctant to include worker demands (feared alienating industrialists).
Women:
Large-scale participation (salt march, protests, manufactured salt, picketed shops, went to jail). From high-caste urban & rich peasant rural families. Saw service to nation as sacred duty. BUT increased public role didn't mean radical change in position; Congress reluctant to give authority positions.

Limits of Civil Disobedience:

Dalits ('Untouchables' / Harijans):

Congress ignored dalits initially (fear of offending sanatanis). Gandhi declared Swaraj impossible without eliminating untouchability, called them 'harijan' (children of God), organised satyagraha for temple entry/access to wells etc.

Dalit leaders (Dr B.R. Ambedkar) sought different political solution: reserved seats, separate electorates. Clashed with Gandhi at 2nd RTC.

Gandhi fasted against separate electorates (believed would slow integration).

Poona Pact (Sep 1932): Reserved seats for Depressed Classes (Scheduled Castes) in provincial/central councils, but voted by general electorate. Dalit participation in CDM limited; remained apprehensive of Congress.

Muslims:

Lukewarm response from some political organisations. Felt alienated from Congress after decline of NCM-Khilafat movement. Congress seemed more associated with Hindu groups (Hindu Mahasabha) from mid-1920s.

Worsening Hindu-Muslim relations; riots deepened distance.

Efforts to renegotiate alliance (1927) failed over representation issue. Muhammad Ali Jinnah (Muslim League) willing to give up separate electorates IF reserved seats in Central Assembly & proportional representation in Muslim-majority provinces assured. Hopes disappeared when Hindu Mahasabha (M.R. Jayakar) opposed compromise.

Atmosphere of suspicion/distrust during CDM. Many Muslims couldn't respond to united struggle call. Feared minority culture/identity submerged under Hindu majority. Sir Muhammad Iqbal (1930) reiterated importance of separate electorates for Muslim minority interests.

5. The Sense of Collective Belonging

Nationalism spreads when people believe they are part of same nation, discover unity. Came partly through united struggles, but also cultural processes capturing imagination.

Icons and Symbols:

Bharat Mata Image:

Identity of India visually associated with image of Bharat Mata (Mother India) from 20th C.

First created by Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay (wrote 'Vande Mataram' hymn in 1870s, included in Anandamath).

Abanindranath Tagore painted famous image (1905) during Swadeshi movement (ascetic figure: calm, divine, spiritual).

Image acquired different forms, circulated in popular prints. Devotion seen as proof of nationalism.

Flags:

  • Swadeshi movement (Bengal): Tricolour (red, green, yellow) designed. 8 lotuses (8 provinces), crescent moon (Hindus/Muslims).
  • By 1921: Gandhiji designed Swaraj flag (tricolour: red, green, white) with spinning wheel in centre (Gandhian ideal of self-help). Carrying flag became symbol of defiance.

Folklore Revival:

Late 19th C: Nationalists recorded folk tales/songs sung by bards, toured villages. Believed these gave true picture of traditional culture corrupted by outside forces. Essential to preserve folk tradition to discover national identity, restore pride.

Bengal: Rabindranath Tagore collected ballads, nursery rhymes, myths.

Madras: Natesa Sastri published Tamil folk tales ('The Folklore of Southern India'). Believed folklore was national literature.

Reinterpretation of History:

Late 19th C: Indians felt need to reinterpret history to instill pride. British saw Indians as backward/primitive. Indians looked to past glorious achievements (art, architecture, science, maths, religion, culture, law, philosophy, trade). This glorious time seen as followed by decline under colonisation. Urged readers take pride in past, struggle against miserable present.

Problem: Glorifying Hindu past / using Hindu iconography could leave other communities feeling left out.

Conclusion

Growing anger against colonial government brought diverse groups into common struggle for freedom. Congress under Gandhi tried channeling grievances into organised movements, forging national unity.

BUT diverse groups participated with varied aspirations; freedom meant different things. Congress attempted resolving differences, but unity often broke down (phases of disunity/inner conflict).

Result: A nation emerging with many voices wanting freedom from colonial rule.

Quit India Movement (1942)

Launched by Gandhi after failure of Cripps Mission amid WWII discontent. Called for complete British withdrawal ('Do or Die'). Mass movement involving students, workers, peasants. Leaders like Jayprakash Narayan, Aruna Asaf Ali, Ram Manohar Lohia active. Women like Matangini Hazra (Bengal), Kanaklata Barua (Assam), Rama Devi (Odisha) participated. British responded with force, took over a year to suppress.

×

Mahatma Gandhi

Leader of the Indian independence movement. Returned to India 1915. Developed Satyagraha (truth force/non-violent resistance). Led Champaran, Kheda, Ahmedabad movements. Initiated Non-Cooperation, Civil Disobedience, Quit India movements. Advocated Hindu-Muslim unity and abolition of untouchability ('Harijan').

×

General Dyer

British military commander responsible for the Jallianwala Bagh massacre in Amritsar on April 13, 1919. He ordered troops to fire on an unarmed crowd, aiming to 'produce a moral effect'.

×

Muhammad Ali & Shaukat Ali

Muslim leaders prominent in the Khilafat Movement after WWI. They discussed possibilities of united mass action with Gandhi, leading to the Non-Cooperation-Khilafat movement.

×

Baba Ramchandra

A sanyasi and former indentured labourer from Fiji. Led peasant movement in Awadh against talukdars and landlords, demanding reduction of revenue, abolition of begar, and social boycott.

×

Jawaharlal Nehru

Prominent Congress leader, later India's first Prime Minister. Involved with Awadh peasant movement. Along with Bose, pressed for more radical mass agitation and Purna Swaraj. President of Lahore Congress (1929).

×

Alluri Sitaram Raju

Led militant guerrilla movement of tribal peasants in Gudem Hills (Andhra) in early 1920s. Claimed special powers. Inspired by Gandhi but believed force necessary for liberation. Executed 1924, became folk hero.

×

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

Leader of the Dalits. Organised Depressed Classes Association (1930). Advocated separate electorates for Dalits to ensure political empowerment. Clashed with Gandhi over this issue. Agreed to Poona Pact (1932) securing reserved seats instead.

×

Muhammad Ali Jinnah

Leader of the Muslim League. Initially involved in efforts for Hindu-Muslim unity but later became a strong advocate for separate electorates and Muslim political safeguards, fearing domination by Hindu majority. Key figure in the demand for Pakistan.

×

Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay

Bengali writer. First created the image/concept of Bharat Mata. Wrote the hymn 'Vande Mataram' in the 1870s, later included in his novel Anandamath.

×

Abanindranath Tagore

Artist who painted the famous image of Bharat Mata in 1905, portraying her as an ascetic, calm, divine figure, inspired by the Swadeshi movement.

×

1915: Gandhi Returns

Mahatma Gandhi returned to India in January 1915 after years in South Africa where he had successfully fought the racist regime using his novel method of mass agitation, Satyagraha. His return marked a new phase in India's struggle for independence.

×

1917: Champaran & Kheda Satyagrahas

Gandhi travelled to Champaran, Bihar, to inspire peasants against the oppressive indigo plantation system. Later that year, he organised a satyagraha in Kheda, Gujarat, to support peasants affected by crop failure and plague, who couldn't pay revenue and demanded collection be relaxed. These were his first major applications of Satyagraha in India.

×

1918: Ahmedabad Mill Satyagraha

Mahatma Gandhi went to Ahmedabad to organise a satyagraha movement among cotton mill workers, intervening in a dispute between workers and mill owners over wages.

×

1919: Rowlatt Act & Jallianwala Bagh

The Anarchical and Revolutionary Crimes Act (Rowlatt Act) was passed, allowing detention without trial. Gandhi called for a hartal. Protests led to violence and repression. On April 13, General Dyer ordered troops to fire on a peaceful gathering in Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar, killing hundreds. The Khilafat Committee was also formed in Bombay (March) to defend the Ottoman Caliph's position.

×

1920: Non-Cooperation Adopted

At the Congress session in Nagpur (December 1920), after much debate, the Non-Cooperation programme was adopted. This followed Gandhi convincing leaders at the Calcutta session (September) of the need to support Khilafat and demand Swaraj through non-cooperation.

×

1921: NCM Launched

The Non-Cooperation-Khilafat Movement began in January 1921. It saw widespread participation from various social groups across India, involving boycotts of schools, courts, councils, and foreign goods, although interpretations of Swaraj varied.

×

1922: Chauri Chaura & NCM Withdrawal

In February 1922, at Chauri Chaura in Gorakhpur, UP, a peaceful demonstration turned violent, clashing with police. Hearing of the incident, Mahatma Gandhi decided to withdraw the Non-Cooperation Movement, feeling it was turning violent and satyagrahis needed more training for mass struggles.

×

1924: Raju Arrested

Alluri Sitaram Raju, who led the militant tribal uprising in the Gudem Hills of Andhra Pradesh, was captured and executed by the British. His arrest marked the end of this two-year armed struggle, though he became a folk hero.

×

1927: FICCI Formed

To organise business interests and react against colonial policies restricting trade, prominent industrialists like Purshottamdas Thakurdas and G.D. Birla formed the Federation of the Indian Chamber of Commerce and Industries (FICCI).

×

1928: Simon Commission & Bardoli

The all-British Statutory Commission under Sir John Simon arrived in India to review the constitutional system, met with widespread protests ('Go back Simon'). In Bardoli, Gujarat, Vallabhbhai Patel led a successful peasant satyagraha against enhancement of land revenue.

×

1929: Purna Swaraj Demand

In October, Viceroy Lord Irwin made a vague offer of 'dominion status'. Unsatisfied, the Congress session in Lahore (December), under Jawaharlal Nehru's presidency, formalised the demand for 'Purna Swaraj' or complete independence. January 26, 1930 was declared as Independence Day.

×

1930: Salt March & CDM Begins

After Irwin ignored Gandhi's 11 demands (including abolishing the salt tax), Gandhi began the Salt March from Sabarmati to Dandi (March 12 - April 6). Violating the salt law at Dandi launched the Civil Disobedience Movement. Dr B.R. Ambedkar also established the Depressed Classes Association this year.

×

1931: Gandhi-Irwin Pact & RTC

On March 5, the Gandhi-Irwin Pact was signed. Gandhi agreed to suspend the Civil Disobedience Movement and participate in the Second Round Table Conference in London; the government agreed to release political prisoners. Gandhi attended the conference in December, but negotiations failed.

×

1932: CDM Relaunched & Poona Pact

Returning from London to find renewed repression, Gandhi relaunched the Civil Disobedience Movement. In September, after Gandhi's fast against separate electorates for Dalits, the Poona Pact was signed between Gandhi and Ambedkar, reserving seats for the Depressed Classes within the general electorate.

×

1934: CDM Loses Momentum

After being relaunched in 1932, the Civil Disobedience Movement continued for over a year but gradually lost its momentum and effectively ended by 1934.

×

1942: Quit India Movement

Launched in August after the failure of the Cripps Mission, Gandhi gave the call 'Do or Die', demanding immediate British withdrawal. It became a mass movement involving widespread hartals, demonstrations, and attacks on state machinery, met with severe repression.
