The Making of a Global World

Class 10 NCERT History Chapter 3

The term "globalization" refers to the increasing integration and interdependence of different economies, societies, and cultures across the world. This chapter examines how the modern global world emerged through a long history of trade, migration, and movements of capital, goods, and ideas across countries.

Pre-Modern World

Early trade routes, migrations and movements that connected different parts of the world before European colonization.

The 19th Century

The age of industrialization, colonization, and the emergence of a world economy dominated by the West.

Inter-War Economy

The economic aftermath of World War I, the Great Depression, and its global impact on trade and finance.

Post-War Era

Rebuilding the world economy after World War II, the Bretton Woods system, and the new international economic order.

1. The Pre-Modern World

The making of the global world has a long history, starting with ancient civilizations. As early as 3000 BCE, an active coastal trade linked the Indus Valley civilizations with present-day West Asia.

Silk Routes Link the World

The silk routes are a prime example of pre-modern trade and cultural links between distant parts of the world. These routes connected Asia, Europe, and northern Africa. Chinese silk cargoes traveled through these routes, as did gold and silver from Europe to Asia.

These routes were used not just for trading goods but also for cultural exchanges. Chinese pottery, textiles and spices from India and Southeast Asia found their way into Europe. Buddhism came to flourish in many parts of East and Southeast Asia through these routes.

Key Insight: The silk routes are named after the Chinese silk cargoes that traveled along this route, but many other goods were also traded.

The Silk Routes connected distant parts of the world

Food Travels: Spaghetti and Potato

Food offers fascinating examples of long-distance cultural exchange. Many of our common foods came from outside through trade or conquest:

Noodles traveled west from China

It is believed that noodles traveled from China to become the Italian 'spaghetti'. Arab traders took away pasta from Italy, adding to their own cuisine so that it traveled eastward once again.

Potatoes, Chillies and Tomatoes

Common foods such as potatoes, soya, groundnuts, maize, tomatoes, chillies, and sweet potatoes were not known to our ancestors until about five centuries ago. These came from America after Columbus discovered it in 1492.

Conquest, Disease and Trade

European conquest and colonization of the Americas dramatically altered the world. The discovery of vast lands and abundant sources of minerals substantially transformed trade and lives everywhere.

Precious metals like silver from mines in present-day Peru and Mexico enhanced European wealth and sparked global trade. Chinese merchants were so eager to secure silver that they would accept it exclusively as payment for their goods.

The Portuguese and Spanish conquests in America were followed by Europeans' commercial interest. This led to the slave tradeBetween the 16th and 19th centuries, millions of African slaves were shipped to America to work on plantations, following the decimation of native communities. and the establishment of plantations in the Americas as well as in parts of Asia, producing sugarcane, cotton, and several other crops almost exclusively for export to Europe.

The Devastating Impact of Germs

The most powerful weapon of European colonization was not guns or warfare, but germs such as those of smallpox that they carried. The native communities had no immunity against these diseases that came with the Europeans. These diseases killed and decimated whole communities, paving the way for foreign conquest.

By 1600, America's original inhabitants had nearly been exterminated – they were down to about 3 percent of the population they had been in 1492.

2. The Nineteenth Century (1815-1914)

The nineteenth century marked a significant period in global economic history. It saw the rise of the modern industrial economy and the establishment of a complex global network of trade, labor, and capital.

A World Economy Takes Shape

By the 1890s, a global agricultural economy had taken shape. Now food no longer came from a nearby village or town, but from far away places. For example, countries like Eastern Europe, Russia, America, and Australia began to export wheat, while meat came from New Zealand and Argentina. Britain began to import food products from abroad and cut food production at home.

Why did Britain import food instead of producing it domestically?

This shift in Britain was made possible by the rapid and cheap transportation of food grains. The introduction of refrigerated ships also enabled distant countries to export perishable items like meat, butter, and eggs. Additionally, Britain had abolished the Corn Laws in 1846, removing tariff protection for agriculture, which made it more economical to import food than to produce it domestically.

Growth of global agricultural trade in the 19th century

Role of Technology

Technology played a crucial role in transforming the nineteenth-century world:

Railways

The railways, introduced in the 1830s, linked the interiors of continents. By 1875, railways were crisscrossing Europe and America, opening up new lands for settlement and enabling speedy and inexpensive transfer of goods.

Steamships

Steamships reduced the journey time across the Atlantic from months to weeks. The Suez Canal, built in Egypt with French capital and engineering skills, linked Europe to Asia, reducing the distance between London and Bombay by 7,000 kilometers.

Telegraph

The telegraph, patented in the USA in 1837, made almost instantaneous communication possible. By 1872, four cables connected Europe and North America. The cost of sending messages also reduced dramatically.

Late Nineteenth-century Colonialism

The late nineteenth century witnessed the rise of colonialism, as European powers competed for territories in Asia and Africa:

In the late nineteenth century, European powers began a scramble for territorial possessions in Africa, driven by various economic and political factors. Britain and France made vast additions to their overseas territories. Belgium and Germany also became new colonial powers.

By the end of the century, Britain had emerged as the world's leading economy, with its political and economic power allowing it to influence events across the globe. The British empire became the largest empire the world had ever seen, controlling colonies across Asia and Africa.

India's Role in the World Economy

India played a crucial role in the late-nineteenth-century world economy. Britain had a trade surplus with India, which helped offset its deficits with other regions. India also provided a large market for British manufactures and served as a source of raw materials and foodgrains. Additionally, Indian traders, laborers, and entrepreneurs became part of the global network of trade and finance.

Rinderpest, or the Cattle Plague

The global connectedness in the nineteenth century had unforeseen consequences. One example was the spread of the rinderpestA fast-spreading disease of cattle, also known as cattle plague. The word 'rinderpest' is German and means 'cattle-plague'. or cattle plague.

In the 1890s, a fast-spreading disease of cattle, the rinderpest (also known as the cattle plague), arrived in Africa. It was carried by infected cattle imported from British Asia to feed the Italian soldiers invading Eritrea in East Africa. The disease spread like wildfire in Africa and killed 90% of the cattle.

The loss of cattle destroyed African livelihoods. Planters, mine owners, and colonial governments took advantage of the situation to push Africans into the labor market. Control over the scarce resource of cattle enabled Europeans to colonize Africa.

Indentured Labor Migration from India

In the nineteenth century, thousands of Indian and Chinese laborers went to work on plantations, in mines, and in road and railway construction projects around the world.

In India, indentured laborers were hired under contracts which promised return passage to India after they had worked for five years on plantations. Most indentured workers came from the present-day regions of eastern Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, central India, and the dry districts of Tamil Nadu.

Numerous factors led to this migration. In the mid-nineteenth century, these regions experienced many social and economic changes – cottage industries declined, land rents rose, lands were cleared for mines and plantations, and increasingly peasants found it difficult to pay their revenue.

Agents (or recruiters) were paid a small commission by the employers, and they used all kinds of methods to recruit laborers. Many migrants agreed to take up work hoping to escape poverty or oppression in their home villages. While some went in search of work willingly, others were forcibly recruited.

Indian indentured laborers working on plantations abroad

Important Note: The living and working conditions for indentured laborers were often harsh. Many laborers were treated like slaves, and though they had the right to return, many chose to stay on, creating new Indian diaspora communities across the world, particularly in the Caribbean islands, Fiji, Mauritius, and South Africa.

3. The Inter-War Economy

The First World War (1914-18) was a devastating event that fundamentally transformed the global economic and political landscape. It marked the end of an era of relative peace and prosperity that had characterized much of the nineteenth century.

Wartime Transformations

The First World War saw the use of machine guns, tanks, aircraft, chemical weapons, etc., on a massive scale. These were all increasingly products of modern large-scale industry. To fight the war, millions of soldiers had to be recruited from around the world and moved to the frontlines on large ships and trains.

The war transformed the US from being an international debtor to an international creditor. The war led to the snapping of economic links between some of the world's largest economic powers which were now fighting each other to pay for them. Britain borrowed large sums of money from US banks as well as the US public.

The war led to an economic boom, as industries were rapidly set up to produce war-related goods. This led to a large increase in demand, production, and employment. But the war also caused immense economic hardship, particularly for populations in Europe.

First World War transformed global economics and politics

Impact on India

In India, the war years were years of great hardship:

  • Defense expenditure was financed by war loans and increasing taxes
  • Customs duties were raised and income tax introduced
  • Prices doubled between 1913 and 1918, leading to extreme economic hardship for common people
  • Villages were called upon to supply soldiers for the war, and forced recruitment in rural areas caused widespread anger
  • Crop failures in 1918-19 and 1920-21, coupled with an influenza epidemic, led to famine-like conditions

Post-war Recovery

After the war, Britain faced a prolonged crisis. It took Britain decades to repay the war debt to the US. This affected the world economy in general and Britain in particular.

Prior to the war, Britain was the world's leading economy. However, the war changed this position. While Britain was preoccupied with war, industries developed in Japan and India. After the war, Britain found it difficult to compete with Japan internationally, and with USA in the markets.

The pre-war global financial system collapsed and was replaced by a new one. The US dollar replaced the British pound sterling as the leading international currency. The USA and its citizens owned more overseas assets than foreign governments and citizens owned in the US.

Post-war Agricultural Crisis

After the war, the agricultural sector experienced a deeper and more prolonged crisis:

  • During the war, agricultural production expanded dramatically to meet food demand
  • After the war, grain prices fell, rural incomes declined, and farmers fell deeper into debt
  • Many countries tried to protect their economies through higher tariffs, but this further affected incomes from agriculture

Rise of Mass Production and Consumption

Ford's assembly line production technique

The US economy resumed its expansion in the 1920s, based on mass production techniques and mass consumption patterns:

Mass Production

Henry Ford's assembly-line production method revolutionized manufacturing. By moving the car on a conveyor belt, each worker performed a single task repeatedly. This increased efficiency and reduced labor costs, enabling the affordable Model T Ford.

Mass Consumption

Higher wages enabled workers to afford consumer goods, creating a cycle of higher productivity, incomes, and consumption. Advertisements, department stores, mail-order marketing, and installment credit made mass consumption possible.

This economic boom in the US in the 1920s created the basis of prosperity in the West for the next two decades. But this prosperity was built on fragile foundations.

The Great Depression

The Great Depression began around 1929 and lasted till the mid-1930s. During this period, most parts of the world experienced catastrophic declines in production, employment, incomes, and trade.

The depression was caused by several factors:

  • Agricultural overproduction remained a problem, leading to a fall in agricultural prices and rural incomes
  • Many US banks had lent large amounts to European countries during and after the war, and as these countries struggled to repay, the banks were on the verge of collapse
  • The withdrawal of US loans affected the economies of the borrowing countries
  • In the USA itself, as unemployment soared, purchasing power declined, leading to a fall in demand and further unemployment

The US attempt to protect its economy through high tariffs (the infamous Smoot-Hawley tariff of 1930) worsened the situation. Other industrial countries also raised their tariffs in retaliation. This led to a drastic decline in world trade.

India and the Great Depression

The depression immediately affected Indian trade. India's exports and imports nearly halved between 1928 and 1934. Agriculture prices fell sharply, but the colonial government refused to reduce revenue demands. Peasants and farmers suffered more than urban dwellers. Though agricultural prices fell sharply, the prices of manufactured goods didn't decline as much, so the countryside suffered a greater decline in purchasing power.

Interestingly, British industries were hit badly by the Depression. But British imports into India declined less sharply than imports from other countries. This was because of Britain's colonial relationship with India, which allowed them to safeguard their economic interests at the expense of the colony.

4. Rebuilding a World Economy: The Post-War Era

The Second World War (1939-45) caused immense destruction of lives and property. It also marked the end of European dominance in the world, with the US and the USSR emerging as the new superpowers. The post-war years saw an attempt to rebuild the global economy on a more stable basis.

Post-War Settlement and the Bretton Woods Institutions

Economists and politicians drew two key lessons from inter-war economic experiences:

  1. An industrial society needs to find ways of increasing mass purchasing power of the people—through higher wages, social security, and other schemes such as unemployment insurance
  2. The world economy needs a stable financial system and more cooperation among nations for economic growth

To achieve these goals, the United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference was held in July 1944 at Bretton Woods in New Hampshire, USA. The Bretton Woods Conference established:

International Monetary Fund (IMF)

Designed to deal with external surpluses and deficits of its member nations and preserve economic stability. It would monitor exchange rates and lend to nations with payment difficulties.

International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (World Bank)

Created to finance post-war reconstruction, with an emphasis on economic development, particularly in former colonies.

The post-war international economic system is also often described as the Bretton Woods system. The IMF and the World Bank are referred to as the Bretton Woods institutions or sometimes as the Bretton Woods twins.

The Bretton Woods Conference established the post-war economic order

Key Feature: The Bretton Woods system was based on fixed exchange rates. National currencies were pegged to the US dollar at a fixed exchange rate, and the dollar itself was anchored to gold at a fixed price of $35 per ounce of gold. This system lasted until 1971.

The Early Post-war Years

The Bretton Woods system inaugurated an era of unprecedented growth of trade and incomes for the Western industrial nations and Japan. World trade grew annually at over 8 percent between 1950 and 1970, and incomes at nearly 5 percent.

The economic recovery of West Europe and Japan was supported by the US through the Marshall PlanThe European Recovery Programme (popularly known as the Marshall Plan after the US Secretary of State who announced it) under which the US provided financial aid to rebuild Western European economies after the end of World War II.. Europe and Japan were rapidly rebuilt through the recovery plan, and they became major trade partners with the US.

International economic institutions like the IMF and World Bank were often dominated by developed countries like the US, who had maximum voting rights. These institutions governed the post-war economic order through various practices and rules.

Decolonization and Independence

The post-war era also saw the independence of many Asian and African countries that had been under European colonial rule. These newly independent countries faced the challenge of economic development in a world dominated by the US and influenced by the Cold War dynamics.

Many former colonies and developing countries did not benefit from the economic growth that the developed world experienced. They faced acute economic challenges like low per capita incomes, poverty, lack of industrialization, and dependence on primary exports.

End of Bretton Woods and the Beginning of Globalization

Despite the economic stability provided by the Bretton Woods system, it couldn't last forever. From the 1960s, the rising costs of the US's overseas involvements, including the Vietnam War, led to a growing deficit in the US. The US dollar, which had become the global currency, was now no longer respected.

In 1971, US President Nixon removed the gold backing from the dollar, effectively ending the Bretton Woods system. Currencies were now allowed to float against each other, leading to a period of financial instability and crisis.

The 1970s also witnessed the beginning of what we recognize as 'globalization' today. The world experienced a deeper level of financial interconnectedness, with MNCs (Multinational Corporations) playing a big role. International financial flows increased rapidly as restrictions on foreign investment and trade were relaxed.

The most recent phase of globalization (sometimes called 'late globalization') arrived in the late 1970s and 1980s, driven by:

  • Information technology revolution
  • Development of satellite communication and fiber optics
  • Internet and electronic banking
  • New trade agreements reducing barriers to commerce
  • Rise of MNCs with global reach and production facilities

Modern globalization connects economies across the world

India in the Post-War Global Economy

India's own position in the post-war world economy was complex. As a newly independent country in 1947, India had to decide how to engage with the international economic order.

India's Economic Strategy

India adopted a mixed economic strategy after independence. While engaging with international trade and seeking foreign investment in certain sectors, it also followed a policy of self-reliance and import substitution industrialization. This approach sought to build domestic industrial capacity while limiting dependence on foreign goods and capital.

India's first Prime Minister, Jawaharlal Nehru, along with statistician P.C. Mahalanobis, formulated a strategy focused on developing heavy industries, which was reflected in India's five-year plans. However, India's share in world trade declined in the decades after independence, and the country faced persistent balance of payments problems.

In the 1990s, following a serious economic crisis, India began liberalizing its economy, reducing barriers to trade and investment, and integrating more closely with the global economy. This marked a shift away from the earlier policy of self-reliance towards greater participation in globalization.

The most recent phase of globalization, beginning from around the 1970s, was made possible largely due to the development of information and communication technologies (ICT) like satellite communication, the Internet, and global mobile networking. These technologies compressed time and space, facilitating almost real-time financial transactions and coordination of production across countries.

Glossary of Key Terms

Corn Laws

Refer to the laws introduced in Britain to protect its agriculture by imposing heavy duty on imported grains. These were abolished in 1846, marking Britain's shift toward free trade policies.

Rinderpest

A fast-spreading disease of cattle, also known as cattle plague. The word 'rinderpest' is German and means 'cattle-plague'. It caused widespread devastation in Africa in the 1890s.

Indentured Labor

A system where laborers were hired under contracts which promised return passage after they had worked for a specified period (usually five years) on plantations. Many Indians migrated as indentured laborers in the 19th century.

Great Depression

A severe worldwide economic depression that began in the US in 1929 and lasted until the late 1930s. It was the longest and most severe depression experienced by the industrialized Western world.

Bretton Woods System

The international monetary system established after World War II, named after the location of the conference (Bretton Woods, New Hampshire) where it was designed. It established fixed exchange rates with the US dollar as the key currency.

Marshall Plan

The European Recovery Programme (popularly known as the Marshall Plan after the US Secretary of State who announced it) under which the US provided financial aid to rebuild Western European economies after the end of World War II.

Tariff

A tax or duty to be paid on particular classes of imports or exports. Tariffs are often used to protect domestic industries from foreign competition.

Globalization

The process of increasing interaction and integration among people, companies, and governments worldwide. It has accelerated since the late 20th century due to advances in transportation and communication technology.

Study Tips & Exam Preparation

Key Points to Remember

  • The silk routes connected Asia, Europe, and northern Africa, facilitating trade and cultural exchange in the pre-modern world.
  • European colonization of the Americas led to the exchange of goods, crops, people, and diseases, profoundly transforming both the Americas and Europe.
  • The 19th century saw the formation of a global agricultural economy, facilitated by technology like railways, steamships, and telegraphs.
  • Indentured labor migration from India was a significant phenomenon in the 19th century, with laborers going to work in plantations across the world.
  • The First World War caused economic disruption, leading to changes in the global financial system with the US replacing Britain as the leading economic power.
  • The Great Depression (1929-1930s) caused worldwide economic hardship, affecting trade, production, employment, and incomes.
  • The Bretton Woods Conference (1944) established the IMF and World Bank to help stabilize the post-war global economy.
  • The period from the 1970s onwards saw a new phase of globalization, driven by technological advances and changes in economic policies.
